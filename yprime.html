YPrime: Designing for everyone — Foundational design principles for a clinical research tool.

2022 — 2023

CHALLENGE

30% of clinical trial participants drop out before completion. Re-recruiting a single patient costs $19,000. The tools they used daily were never designed for them.

STRATEGY

I led the design of a research-driven eCOA platform built on emotional neutrality as a clinical requirement and has to serve users aged 14 to 90 across 250+ languages with zero behavioural nudging.

RESULTS

Completion rates rose, trial abandonment dropped, and study setup time compressed, while participants from rural Poland to urban Nigeria felt the platform was built for them.

CHALLENGE

The black box of eCOA

The tools participants used every day were never designed for them. And nobody could tell, because in this industry, nobody talks. I led the design of a research-driven eCOA platform built on one foundational idea: emotional neutrality is not a design preference in clinical research. It is a clinical requirement. The platform had to serve users aged eight to ninety, across 250+ languages, with zero behavioural nudging.

STRATEGY

Research-driven design

Behind every decision, every animation, layout pattern, piece of copy there was a research. Qualitative interviews, usability testing, quantitative surveys, and exploratory studies across geographies and ability profiles. Bias is invisible. In clinical trials, even small friction can mean lost data or broken trust. We did not validate assumptions. We questioned them.

Identifying the gaps

Understanding the backbone of the project came before any screen work. How does the flow look for the patient, the doctor, the study coordinator, the caregiver? What data passes to the server? What errors can occur from connection loss? Which APIs are in play, where do they go, and does the user need feedback while that happens? Every actor in the system was mapped through their own journey before a single shared component was drawn.

Designing for perceptual redundancy

Colour in clinical UX carries more weight than in almost any other context. Red signals emergency. Sterile blue reads as cold and clinical. Green implies clearance. We needed something that arrived without baggage, a colour participants would encounter daily for months without triggering an association that could quietly skew how they reported their symptoms.

We tested over 100 combinations against emotional keyword associations and AAA contrast standards. We landed on a calm, mid-spectrum purple — chosen not because it looked good in a presentation, but because it evoked nothing in particular. In this context, that restraint is exactly the point.

💡 A useful test: blur all the text and remove colour. If the interface still communicates its purpose, it is working.

Shape carried equal weight. Every core function — confirming, navigating, submitting — was defined by predictable placement, distinct geometry, and clear affordance, with colour as a reinforcing layer rather than the primary signal.

Designing for modularity, not personalization

Every ECOA user is unique by age, language, country, and device. But so is every clinical study. The challenge was building a system flexible enough for research variation while remaining emotionally consistent for participants. Everything was broken into modules with clear groupings and variations. Progress indicators, text alignment, copyright attribution per screen, branching logic, input types: each one configurable by the research team, invisible in its complexity to the participant.

Scalability

One of the earliest challenges was adaptability, not just technical performance. The app needed to work across screen sizes, system settings, and languages in a real way: accommodating long labels, different reading directions, and regional interface norms. Internationalisation touched every level of the design system, from type hierarchies that support complex character sets to layout logic that respects bidirectional text. The result is a product that adapts not only technically, but culturally.

Spatial logic and cognitive load

Interaction had to feel grounded and intuitive. We avoided flashy transitions that could confuse or impress, instead designing motion that mirrors real-world metaphors. Screens slide like paper across a desk. Surveys drop down like a document handed to you and lift away when complete. Pages of equal hierarchy slide horizontally, aligned with their place in navigation. These transitions form a consistent mental map: users always know where they are and what comes next.

The homepage is the clearest expression of the philosophy. It shows exactly what is due today. If nothing is scheduled, the space stays empty — deliberately. In an attention economy built on filling every moment, that silence is a design decision that takes real conviction to defend.

The Engagement Play-book (and Why We Set It Aside)

Stakeholders raised gamification early. It was a reasonable instinct. We took it seriously. We tested progress rings, completion streaks, and compliance scores, etc. across participant cohorts. Then we set every one of them aside.

The problem is not that these mechanics fail to drive behaviour. It is that the behaviour they drive is the wrong kind. A participant chasing a streak is no longer reporting how they genuinely feel. They are performing compliance. In clinical research, clean data is worth more than high engagement. We built the argument in research, presented it with data, and held the line.

What we kept was the quietest possible form of positive reinforcement: a small, non-interactive badge after task or training completion. No sound. No animation. No demand for acknowledgement. Just a brief signal of closure. Even this was tested extensively across cultural contexts, not for delight, but for emotional safety.

eConsent

Alongside the core platform, I designed eConsent from the ground up, taking it from an initial concept to a fully architected, validated, developer-ready product. This was not a digital signature tool. It was a framework for genuine comprehension.

Participants can review study documents section by section at their own pace, highlight passages, annotate questions, and flag anything before signing. The system supports multi-party consent for minors, reconsent when protocols change mid-trial, and medical consultation during review. Every feature was built around one principle: no signature before genuine understanding.

RESULTS

Clean data for years to come

The yPrime platform launched with 250+ languages and full FDA and EMA regulatory approval. Greater interface clarity led to a measurable decrease in support calls from confused participants. Daily task completion rates climbed while missed submissions fell compared to the previous legacy platform. We built a system that bridges different generations and geographies, and gives researchers a tool they can trust to collect clean data for years to come.
